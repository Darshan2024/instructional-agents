Target Audience & Needs Analysis
================================

### **1. Comprehensive Profile of Target Students: "Python Fundamentals"**

This profile synthesizes pedagogical analysis and institutional data to describe the students, their needs, and the instructional strategies required for success in this foundational course.

#### **Audience Composition & Prior Knowledge**

*   **Profile:** The course serves a large, academically diverse undergraduate population. Institutional data shows a majority (**65%**) are from outside Computer Science, with enrollment breaking down as:
    *   **~40% Arts & Sciences** (STEM, Social Sciences, Digital Humanities)
    *   **~35% Engineering & Computer Science** (primarily first-year majors)
    *   **~15% School of Business** (Finance, Business Analytics)
    *   **~10% Other/Undeclared**
*   **Prior Knowledge:** The course is designed for **true beginners with zero programming experience.** Students are assumed to have general computer literacy but lack knowledge of programming concepts (variables, loops, functions), mental models for code execution, and experience with development environments. Historical student feedback confirms that local software installation is a significant barrier, reinforcing the need to assume no prior experience with technical setups.
*   **Motivations:** Student motivations are largely pragmatic and discipline-specific. For CS majors, it is a mandatory prerequisite. For the non-CS majority, the primary goal is acquiring a practical tool for data analysis, research, and automation to enhance their skills and employability in their chosen field (e.g., biology, economics, linguistics).

#### **Key Learning Needs & Anticipated Challenges**

Based on faculty analysis and recurring themes in past course evaluations, students face several predictable challenges:

1.  **Logistical & Environmental Friction:** Data shows the most common historical frustration is with software installation and environment setup, which creates a major barrier to learning before any code is written and disproportionately affects students with less technical confidence.
2.  **Abstract to Concrete Translation:** The primary conceptual hurdle is translating a problem description into a precise, logical sequence of steps (an algorithm) *before* writing code.
3.  **Lack of a Mental Model for Execution:** Students struggle to visualize how a computer executes code, tracks the state of variables within loops, and manages the flow of control between functions.
4.  **Low Resilience in Debugging:** Novices often perceive error messages as personal failures rather than diagnostic clues. They lack a systematic process for interpreting errors and fixing code, leading to high frustration and a tendency to get "stuck." This is validated by student feedback highlighting the critical role of TAs in "explaining error messages."

#### **Appropriate Educational Approaches**

The student profile and their learning needs demand a highly structured, supportive, and practical pedagogical approach:

*   **Scaffolded Problem-Solving:** Dedicate class time and assignments to pre-coding activities like writing pseudocode or creating flowcharts to bridge the abstract-to-concrete gap.
*   **"Cloud-First" Environment:** Mandate a single, browser-based IDE to eliminate setup friction, ensure equity, and allow support staff to become experts on a consistent platform. This directly addresses the top historical complaint from students.
*   **Visualize Execution:** Use tools like Python Tutor and explicit instructor narration during live coding to help students build an accurate mental model of program flow and state.
*   **Systematize Debugging:** Teach debugging as a core, non-negotiable skill. Create assignments where students are given broken code to diagnose and fix, normalizing errors as part of the learning process.
*   **Provide Relatable, Diverse Content:** Use examples and projects drawn from a wide range of disciplines (e.g., biology, business, humanities) to engage the 65% non-CS majority and validate their diverse motivations for taking the course.
*   **Leverage Hands-on Support:** Utilize mandatory, well-staffed lab sections, as data shows students consider access to TAs and peer support a crucial element of their success.

### **2. Data-Driven Recommendations for Course Adjustments**

The following recommendations are derived from a synthesis of pedagogical analysis and institutional data, including enrollment trends, DFW rates, and historical student feedback.

1.  **Recommendation: Adopt a "Cloud-First" Policy with a Standardized Cloud-Based IDE.**
    *   **Data Justification:** This is the highest-impact intervention, directly addressing the most common negative theme in past course evaluations: **"Setup & Environment Frustration."** It eliminates a major source of early-semester attrition, ensures an equitable starting point for all students, and mitigates a key factor contributing to the historical **18-25% DFW rate**.

2.  **Recommendation: Implement an Auto-Grading Platform for Foundational Assignments.**
    *   **Data Justification:** This addresses two data-backed issues. First, it solves the scalability problem presented by a **30% enrollment growth** over two years. Second, it directly responds to student feedback complaining of slow and vague feedback on assignments, providing the immediate, consistent, and specific input novices need to learn from mistakes effectively.

3.  **Recommendation: Design a Curriculum with Diverse, Domain-Specific Examples.**
    *   **Data Justification:** Enrollment data confirms that **65% of students are non-CS majors**. A curriculum that reflects applications in science, business, and humanities is essential for maintaining engagement and relevance for the majority of the class, for whom Python is a practical tool, not a theoretical pursuit.

4.  **Recommendation: Formalize Debugging Instruction as a Core Learning Objective.**
    *   **Data Justification:** Course evaluations reveal that students rely heavily on TAs for "explaining error messages." This indicates a critical skill gap. By moving from reactive support to proactive instruction—teaching a formal debugging process—the course can build student resilience, reduce frustration, and better equip them to overcome challenges that contribute to the high DFW rate.

5.  **Recommendation: Protect and Enhance Funding for TA-Led Lab Sections.**
    *   **Data Justification:** The most common positive feedback theme is the value of "Lab & TA Support." This data provides a strong administrative justification to frame investment in TAs not as a cost, but as a proven, high-impact strategy for improving student success and retention in a historically challenging course.

6.  **Recommendation: Establish and Track Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).**
    *   **Data Justification:** Use the historical **18-25% DFW rate as a baseline benchmark.** Track this metric after implementing the proposed changes to measure their impact. Supplement this with targeted questions in end-of-semester evaluations on the effectiveness of the cloud IDE and auto-grader to create a data-driven feedback loop for continuous course improvement.
