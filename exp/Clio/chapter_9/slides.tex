\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Understanding Ethical Dilemmas - Definition}
    An ethical dilemma in software engineering refers to situations where a software engineer faces a conflict between two or more moral principles or ethical standards. 
    These dilemmas often arise when a decision must be made that could negatively impact users, stakeholders, or society, and where no option presents a clear ethical path.
    
    \begin{block}{Key Characteristics}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Conflicting Values:} Competing values, such as user privacy vs. data accessibility.
            \item \textbf{Complex Decision-Making:} Shades of gray where both choices might seem right.
            \item \textbf{Significant Impact:} Choices can have real-world consequences that affect individuals or groups.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Understanding Ethical Dilemmas - Distinction from Other Challenges}
    Ethical dilemmas differ from other challenges in software engineering, such as:
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Technical Challenges:} Issues related to performance that do not involve moral components.
        \item \textbf{Business Challenges:} Decisions focused on project management and budget constraints.
        \item \textbf{Compliance Issues:} Following laws or regulations which are procedural rather than ethical.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \begin{block}{Examples}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Optimizing an algorithm for performance.
            \item Cutting features to meet a deadline.
            \item Adhering to GDPR without ethical considerations.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Examples of Ethical Dilemmas in Software Engineering}
    Some common ethical dilemmas faced by software engineers include:
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{User Privacy vs. Data Utility:} Deciding to collect user data for performance enhancement vs. user privacy.
        \item \textbf{Algorithmic Bias:} Ensuring fairness in machine learning models amidst existing societal biases.
        \item \textbf{Security vs. Accessibility:} Balancing security measures with software accessibility for individuals with disabilities.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \begin{block}{Key Points to Emphasize}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Ethical dilemmas shape the moral landscape of technology.
            \item Recognizing these dilemmas guides responsible decision-making.
            \item They require deeper examination of personal and societal values.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Concepts - Trust}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Definition:} 
        Trust is the confidence users place in a software system regarding its reliability and ethical behavior.
        \item \textbf{Importance:} 
        When users trust software products, they are more likely to engage with them and share sensitive information.
        \item \textbf{Example:} 
        User trust was significantly impacted during the Cambridge Analytica scandal, where users felt their data was mishandled, resulting in a loss of trust in various platforms.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Concepts - Data Privacy}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Definition:} 
        Data privacy pertains to how personal data is collected, used, and shared, ensuring that users' sensitive information remains secure and confidential.
        \item \textbf{Importance:} 
        Adhering to ethical principles promotes transparency, allowing users to control their own data and understand data usage.
        \item \textbf{Example:} 
        Implementing General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) compliant practices demonstrates to users that their privacy is paramount.
        \item \textbf{Consideration:} 
        Engineers should adopt privacy-by-design principles, ensuring privacy considerations are integrated into the development lifecycle.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Concepts - User Safety}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Definition:} 
        User safety ensures that software systems protect users from harm, either physical, psychological, or emotional.
        \item \textbf{Importance:} 
        Building software with user safety in mind not only enhances user experience but also mitigates legal risks and ethical scrutiny.
        \item \textbf{Example:} 
        Autonomous vehicles must be programmed to prioritize passenger and pedestrian safety, integrating fail-safes to prevent accidents.
        \item \textbf{Key Action:} 
        Conduct regular safety assessments and usability testing to identify any potential safety hazards.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Case Study 1: Data Privacy and User Consent}
    \begin{block}{Overview}
        Data privacy refers to the proper handling of sensitive personal information, ensuring that individuals have control over how their data is collected, used, and shared. User consent is a key component, involving users’ agreement to allow organizations to process their data, typically articulated through privacy policies and terms of service.
    \end{block}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Case Study Analysis: Cambridge Analytica}
    \begin{block}{Background}
        In 2018, it was revealed that personal data from millions of Facebook users had been improperly harvested by Cambridge Analytica for political advertising purposes. Users consented to share their data but were often unaware of how it would be used.
    \end{block}
    
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Data Collection:} Unbeknownst to users, the app developed by Cambridge Analytica collected extensive data from users and their friends.
        \item \textbf{Consent Misuse:} The platform's consent mechanisms were criticized for being complex and misleading, leading to unintentional data sharing.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Ethical Implications}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Informed Consent:} Were users genuinely informed about data usage? Complexity made true informed consent difficult.
        \item \textbf{Trust and Responsibility:} Organizations must protect user data and maintain trust, which was eroded due to the incident.
        \item \textbf{Accountability:} Should companies be accountable for breaches of user trust? Ethical engineering involves protecting data in users' best interests.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Points and Discussion}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{User Awareness:} Ethical software engineering prioritizes transparency in data practices.
        \item \textbf{Simplicity in Consent:} Consent processes should be straightforward and understandable.
        \item \textbf{Continuous Review:} Organizations must regularly update data privacy policies to align with legal changes.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \begin{block}{Discussion Questions}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item What are the implications of misleading consent mechanisms on user trust?
            \item How can software engineers advocate for stronger data privacy practices within their organizations?
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Conclusion}
    This case exemplifies the critical balance between technological advancement and ethical responsibility in data management. Software engineers must ensure that user consent is meaningful, protecting individual rights in an increasingly data-driven world.

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Case Study 2: Algorithmic Bias}

    \begin{block}{Understanding Algorithmic Bias}
        Algorithmic bias occurs when an algorithm produces systematically unfair outcomes due to flawed assumptions in machine learning processes, data selection, or training practices. 
        It can lead to discrimination against certain groups based on race, gender, socioeconomic status, or other characteristics.
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Concepts of Algorithmic Bias}

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Data Bias}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Algorithms learn from data, and if this data reflects historical inequalities, the algorithm will perpetuate them.
            \item \textit{Example:} A hiring algorithm trained primarily on male resumes may favor male candidates unfairly.
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Feedback Loops}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Biased algorithms can create feedback loops that reinforce problematic patterns.
            \item \textit{Example:} A biased predictive policing algorithm may target specific neighborhoods, leading to increased policing and skewed crime data.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Case Study: Facial Recognition Technology}

    \begin{block}{Scenario}
        Facial recognition algorithms have been found to perform significantly better on lighter-skinned individuals than on darker-skinned individuals. 
        A study by MIT Media Lab revealed that certain systems misidentified dark-skinned women 35\% of the time, compared to only 1\% for light-skinned men.
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Ethical Responsibilities of Engineers}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Awareness and Acknowledgment:} Understand that bias can emerge intentionally or unintentionally.
            \item \textbf{Data Stewardship:} Ensure training data is representative and diverse.
            \item \textbf{Transparency:} Provide clarity on algorithmic decisions to users.
            \item \textbf{Testing and Compliance:} Conduct regular audits using fairness metrics.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Key Takeaways}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item Algorithmic bias can unfairly impact lives in areas like hiring, loan approvals, or legal outcomes.
        \item Engineers must incorporate ethical considerations into the software development lifecycle.
        \item Addressing algorithmic bias is a collective responsibility among organizations and society.
    \end{itemize}

    \begin{block}{Conclusion}
        Continuous education, ethical responsibility, and robust oversight are essential to mitigate algorithmic bias in software engineering.
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Frameworks for Ethical Decision Making - Overview}
    Ethical dilemmas in software engineering often arise due to conflicting interests, stakeholder impacts, and the rapid pace of technological advancement.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Frameworks for Ethical Decision Making - Key Decision-Making Frameworks}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Utilitarian Approach}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Evaluates actions based on outcomes to maximize overall happiness or utility.
            \item Example: Weighing user data collection benefits against privacy risks.
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Deontological (Duty-Based) Approach}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Adherence to rules and duties; some actions are morally obligatory.
            \item Example: Rejecting a request to backdoor an application due to ethical violations.
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Virtue Ethics}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Focuses on the character and integrity of the decision-maker.
            \item Example: Advocating for ethical alternatives when faced with socially irresponsible projects.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
    \frametitle{Frameworks for Ethical Decision Making - Stakeholder Theory}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Stakeholder Theory}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Decision-makers should consider all stakeholders' interests.
            \item Example: In designing educational software, gather input from teachers, students, and parents to meet diverse needs.
        \end{itemize}
        
        \item \textbf{Key Points to Emphasize}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Each framework provides a unique perspective; combining them may yield better outcomes.
            \item Continuous learning and discussions about ethics enhance decision-making skills.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
