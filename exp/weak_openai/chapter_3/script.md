### **First Method: Requirement Specification Documents**

**[Pause briefly, then continue]**

The first method is creating a **Requirement Specification Document**. Think of this as a detailed contract—it’s a formal, comprehensive record that describes every aspect of the system's requirements. It covers both **functional requirements**—what the system should do—and **non-functional requirements**, like performance or security standards.

For instance, imagine developing a banking application. The requirement might state: "The system shall allow users to transfer funds between accounts within 10 seconds." This statement is precise, measurable, and testable.

**[Pause]**

Such documents typically include sections like an introduction, scope, stakeholder needs, functional and non-functional requirements, constraints, assumptions, and acceptance criteria. Following standards like IEEE 830 helps ensure consistency and clarity. 

**[Rhetorical Question]**  
Why is this important? Because a clear, well-structured specification reduces ambiguity—so everyone knows exactly what the system must do.

---

### **Next, let's look at User Stories**

**[Advance slide to next frame]**

The second method is **User Stories**. If you've heard of Agile development, you’re familiar with this approach. User stories are short, simple descriptions of a feature from the perspective of the end-user, written in a specific format:

*"As a [user], I want [feature], so that [benefit]."*

For example, "As a customer, I want to view my account balance online, so I can manage my finances conveniently." 

This format keeps focus on delivering value directly aligned with user needs. 

**[Pause]**

User stories are lightweight, easy to write, and promote collaboration among stakeholders. They’re especially useful when requirements are evolving or when working iteratively. 

**[Engagement]**  
Think about your favorite app—what features would you like to see? Writing a user story for that feature makes the needs clear and accessible to everyone.

---

### **Finally, let's consider Prototypes**

**[Advance slide to next frame]**

Prototypes can range from low-fidelity sketches or wireframes to high-fidelity, clickable simulations that nearly resemble the final product. The goal is to bring requirements to life visually, so stakeholders can see and interact with what’s being planned.

**[Pause]**

## Section 6: Techniques for Clear and Complete Requirements

**[Start of script]**

*“Let’s talk about how we can make our system requirements as clear and complete as possible — a crucial foundation for successful project development. When requirements are ambiguous or incomplete, projects often face delays, increased costs, or even failure. So, what techniques can we use to improve the quality of these requirements? Today, I’ll introduce three key methods: IEEE standards, SMART criteria, and traceability matrices.”*

**[Advance to next slide/frame]**

*“First, I want to discuss **IEEE standards**—specifically the guidelines set forth by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers. IEEE provides a well-established framework for documenting requirements, notably through IEEE 830, the Recommended Practice for Software Requirements Specification. These standards emphasize that requirements should be written with clarity, consistency, and traceability in mind. For example, imagine you have a requirement that says, ‘The system shall load data quickly.’ How vague is that? ‘Quickly’ can mean different things to different people. IEEE standards recommend specifying measurable thresholds. So instead, you might say, ‘The system shall load user data within 2 seconds under typical conditions.’ This way, everyone understands what ‘quickly’ actually entails and can verify whether the system meets this requirement during testing.”*

**[Advance to next slide/frame]**

*“Next, let’s explore **SMART criteria**—a popular framework to craft high-quality requirements. SMART is an acronym: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. It ensures each requirement is clear and actionable. For instance, instead of saying ‘Improve system security,’ which is vague and open-ended, you could specify: ‘Implement user authentication with two-factor authentication, or 2FA, for all users within three months.’ This requirement is specific — it states exactly what needs to be done; measurable — it sets a clear deadline; and relevant — it addresses a key security aspect of the system. By applying SMART, your requirements become firmly anchored in reality, significantly reducing misunderstandings and scope creep.”*

**[Advance to next slide/frame]**

*“Finally, let’s examine **traceability matrices**—a vital tool for tracking how requirements relate to design elements, testing, and implementation. Think of a traceability matrix as a detailed map that links each requirement to its corresponding parts of the system. For example, suppose we have a requirement ‘R1: Validate login data,’ and we associate it with the ‘Login Module’ in the design phase, along with a specific test case called ‘TC1’ that checks login validation. By creating a table like this, you can verify at any point whether all requirements are being addressed and easily assess the impact of any proposed changes. If you decide to update the login validation process, the traceability matrix helps you see which parts of the system are affected, ensuring nothing is overlooked.”*

**[Advance to next slide/frame]**

*“To summarize, using IEEE standards guides us in documenting requirements clearly and consistently. Applying SMART criteria helps us formulate requirements that are precise, feasible, and aligned with project goals. And developing traceability matrices early on lets us verify that every requirement is tracked through design and testing, reducing risk and rework. Combining these techniques creates a solid foundation for clear, complete, and actionable requirements — key to successful system development.”*

**[Final slide/frame]**

*“By adopting these methods, teams can communicate expectations more effectively, minimize misunderstandings, and deliver systems that meet stakeholders’ needs accurately. Remember, thorough and precise requirements are the backbone of any successful project. Now, think about your current or future projects: how could applying these techniques improve your requirements process?”*
